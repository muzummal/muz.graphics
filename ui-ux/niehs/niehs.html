Update Top Level Pages to Apply USWDS

Top Level Pages – Design Options

The goal of re-designing the top-level pages was to follow the USWDS as a requirement of U.S. a Government run website. The USWDS goal is to make websites more accessible and mobile-friendly. The stakeholder’s request was also to give users access to lower-level pages.

Option 1 – A clean and modern design concept with most of the emphasis on the child pages. This concept helps users navigate faster to their destination. This option also has special containers to better highlight sections such as Hot Topics.

Option 2 – A straight-forward concept where the emphasis is placed on the child pages two levels down allowing the user a better visual of the site map further increasing navigation. The layout allows the user to understand the hierarchy while keeping the page short which helps them get to their destination faster. This option condenses special containers into call out boxes that reside in a column right of the main content.

Usability Studies – Research, Findings, and Solutions

Comparative Medicine Branch – Standard Operating Procedures Web Application

The Comparative Medicine Branch (CMB) wrote and compiled the Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs). The NIEHS scientists and administrator staff use the search application to filter through the organized SOPs.

Problem - The CMB and other department staff were having trouble using the search application created to filter SOPs.

Research - Our team tested the search application features by observing the scientists and administrator staffs’ usage methods to find SOPs. We observed the staff in a remote setting where they were required to complete a user task list. The users were also asked to share their reasoning on the decisions they made throughout completing the tasks. This allowed us to ask follow-up questions which helped us discover user challenges we had not considered.

Findings - We found that most users used the website's search engine, bookmarked SOP pages, or skipped the application’s search feature to manually search through the SOPs. Furthermore, both users who knew what they were looking for and those who didn’t still avoided the search bar feature.

Pain Points:

  • Uninformed about SOP updates
  • Unable to find SOP revision dates
  • Found search filter options unhelpful

Solutions - The CMB SOP application was updated to include the word “New” or “Updated” as a graphic on new or recently updated SOPs. The revised dates were moved up on the SOP’s page hierarchy below the banner to be more visible. Additionally, we implemented a mailing system to send update notifications to users when they follow an SOP.

The search bar function was improved on the backend to better handle keywords entries. Our team also suggested improving the description of the SOP titles to better match the content.

Lastly, we updated the search filter options to match more precisely with both scientists’ and administrator staffs’ needs. The new list was more accurate because it consisted of suggestions made by both groups.

Top Tasks List

The Top Task list is a modal on a private government webpage that takes users to commonly used tasks and webpages. Both scientists and administrative staff under NIEHS would use the top task list modal to get to their destination faster.

Problem - The Top Task list of items were outdated, inefficient, and ineffective. Users found that it was difficult to understand and find the correct web page.

Research - We interviewed multiple NIEHS scientists and administrative staff. After we discovered a major issue from testing the existing list, we conducted A/B Testing. Group A was tested with a naming convention that listed actions to take and Group B was tested with a naming convention that listed the destination names.

Findings - Users were frustrated with the Top Task list’s links because they were disorganized and poorly written. New users specifically had a hard time with list items that were an abbreviation because they didn’t know what it meant. Group B performed their tasks completion at a much faster rate using the new list in comparison to the original list, and faster than Group A.

Pain Points:

  • Inconsistencies in link’s naming convention
  • Abbreviations were not known to new users

Solution - The solution to these problems were simple and streamlined. First, all the links were changed to the webpage’s title or application’s name. Second, abbreviations were listed in the format: abbreviation dash abbreviation written out. Overall, by incorporating consistency in the naming convention, we reduced the task completion time for users to find their destination.

Recycle Guide

The Recycle Guide was a requested application to help users properly recycle material waste. The criteria for the application were to have the ability to use search filters to find the relevant recyclable materials, and to obtain information on how to recycle those materials.

Problem - NIEHS Staff needed an application that made it easier than the current recycling guide on how to recycle materials on campus.

Research - After multiple discussions with the stakeholders we were able to determine their high-level needs. With that information we created a prototype which we used to test the potential user base.

We examined users with the prototype allowing us to make a few notable discoveries. After a few iterations with the prototype and further observations we were able to tackle pain points as well as solutions that fit the needs of the stakeholder and the NIEHS staff.

Findings - After the scientists and administrative staff used the recycling guide prototype, we identified potential problems. Primarily, the search filters were based on the materials when they should have been based on location because the location changed how and where to recycle materials. We also discovered their need for information about how to dispose materials that cannot be recycled.

Pain Points:

  • Did not know where to recycle material
  • Could not find instructions on disposing nonrecyclables
  • Found irrelevant instructions for their locations

Solutions - After a few iterations and retesting with users and stakeholders, we created separate recyclable and nonrecyclable item cards and provided appropriate instructions. The card provided quick information that was commonly needed and the card’s expansion feature included additional detailed information including how and where to recycle materials.

To further address the recycling location concerns, we adjusted filters based on location. This helped tremendously and reduced the task completion rate during user testing.
