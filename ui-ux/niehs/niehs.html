Update Top Level Pages to Apply USWDS

Top Level Pages – Design Options

The goal of re-designing the top level pages was to follow the USWDS as a requirement of U.S. a Government run website. The USWDS goal is to make websites more accessible and mobile-friendly. The stakeholder’s request was also to give users access to lower level pages.

Option 1 – A clean and modern design concept with most of the emphasis on the child pages. This concept helps users navigate faster to their destination. This option also has special containers to better highlight sections such as Hot Topics.

Option 2 – A straight-forward concept where the emphasis is placed on the child pages two levels down allowing the user a better visual of the site map further increasing navigation. The layout allows the user to understand the hierarchy while keeping the page short which helps them get to their destination faster. This option condenses special containers into call out boxes that reside in a column right of the main content.

Usability Studies – Research, Findings, and Solutions

Comparative Medicine Branch – Standard Operating Procedures Web Application

The Comparative Medicine Branch (CMB) wrote and compiled the Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs). The NIEHS scientist and department administrators use the search application to filter through the organized SOPs.

Problem - The CMB and other department staff were having trouble using the search application created to filter SOPs.

Research - Our team tested the search application features by observing the scientists and administrators usage methods to find SOPs. We observed the staff in a remote setting where they were required to complete a user task list. The users were also asked to share their reasoning on the decisions they made throughout completing the tasks. This allowed us to ask follow up questions which helped us to discover problems we have not considered.

Findings - We found that most users used the website's search engine, bookmarked the page, or skipped the applications search feature to manually search through the list provided by the application. We found that both users who knew what they were looking for and those who didn’t would still avoid the search bar feature.

Pain Points:

  • Uninformed about SOP updates
  • Unable to find SOP revision dates
  • Found search filter options unhelpful

Solutions - The CMB SOP application was updated to include a “New” or “Updated” icon on new or recently updated SOPs. We also changed the SOP pages to clearly show revised dates by moving it higher on the page’s hierarchy. We also implemented a mailing system to send update notifications to users when they follow an SOP.

The search bar function was improved on the backend to better handle keywords entered in the search bar. Also suggestions were made by our team to improve the description of the SOPs’ titles to better match the content.

Lastly, we updated the search filter options to match more precisely with both scientists’ and administrators’ needs. The new list was more accurate because it consisted of suggestions made by both groups.

Top Tasks List

The Top Task list is a list on a private government webpage that takes users to commonly used tasks and webpages. Both scientists and administrative users under NIEHS would use the top task list to get to their destination faster.

Problem - The Top Tasks list has a list of items that users find inconvenient or difficult to find on the list.

Research - We interviewed multiple NIEHS staff that included both scientists and administrators. After we discovered a major issue from testing the original list, we started to do A/B Testing. Group A were tested with a naming convention that listed actions to take and Group B were tested with a naming convention that listed the destination names.

Findings - Users, old and new alike, were frustrated with the Top Task links because they were disorganized and poorly written. Group B performed their task completion at a much faster rate in comparison to the original list and Group A.

Pain Points:

  • Inconsistencies in link’s naming convention
  • Abbreviations were not known to new users

Solution - The solution to these problems were simple and streamlined. First, all the links were changed to the webpage’s title or application’s name. Second, abbreviations were listed in the format: abbreviation dash abbreviation written out. With consistency and a good naming convention, the task list reduced time to complete tasks effectively.

Recycle Guide

The Recycle Guide was a requested application to help users properly recycle material waste. The criteria for the application was to have the ability to use search filters to find the relevant recyclable materials and obtain information on how to recycle those materials.

Problem - NIEHS Staff needed an application that made it easier than the current recycling guide on how to recycle materials on campus.

Research - After multiple discussions with the stakeholders we were able to determine the users’ high level needs. With that information we had created a prototype which we used to test the potential user base.

We examined users with the prototype allowing us to make a few notable discoveries. After a few iterations with the prototype and further observations we were able to tackle pain points as well as solutions that fit the needs of the stakeholder and the NIEHS staff.

Findings - The scientist who would use the recycling guide prototype brought up concerns that have not been identified. Primarily, the search filters and recycling instructions for used materials were not as helpful because the location changed factors in the recycling process. Also we learned it would be helpful to know how to dispose items that cannot be recycled.

Pain Points:

  • Did not know where to recycle material
  • Could not find instructions on disposing nonrecyclables
  • Were confused by instructions because it did not match work environment

Solutions - After a few iterations and retesting with users and stakeholders, we separated recyclable and nonrecyclable items and provided appropriate instructions to each card. The card itself provided quick information that was commonly needed and the card’s expansion feature included additional detailed information including how and where to recycle materials.

To address the recycling location concerns, we adjusted filters based on location. This helped tremendously and reduced the task completion rate during user testing.
